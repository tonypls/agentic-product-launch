# Tone Guide

How co-written launch copy should sound. The co-writing guide covers what to draw out of the builder; this file covers the words that end up on the page. Apply it to every public-facing draft: channel copy, outreach, replies, release notes, video scripts, and recaps.

The goal is copy a reader would attribute to a person, not a program. Launch copy usually fails tone in one of four ways: it sounds like marketing instead of the builder, it leans on tired words, it narrates its own good behavior, or it leaks the playbook's internal vocabulary. Each has a fix below.

## Default Register

Before any voice-matching, start here:

- Write the way the builder would explain the thing to a peer, out loud. If a sentence would be embarrassing to say across a table, rewrite it.
- Confidence comes from specifics — numbers, names, what actually happened — never from adjectives.
- Plain verbs over promotional ones: "it checks the repo" beats "it intelligently audits your repository."
- Short sentences are fine. Fragments are fine. Polish is not the goal; recognizability is.

## Reference Copy: Match How The Builder Actually Writes

The strongest tone signal is writing the builder has already done. Ask for it in the voice interview.

**Collect.** 3–10 pieces the builder wrote themselves — old launch posts, tweets or threads, README intros, blog paragraphs, newsletter issues, even a long message to another builder. Recent beats old, public-facing beats private, and same-channel beats different-channel (their real tweets are the best reference for their X copy). Paste them into the Reference Copy section of `templates/launch-brief.md`.

**Mine.** Read the samples for habits and write a tone profile of 5–8 observations into the launch brief:

- How they open — question, scene, blunt statement, greeting or none.
- Sentence length and rhythm: long and winding, short and clipped, or mixed.
- Punctuation and casing habits: em dashes, parentheses, ellipses, lowercase, exclamation marks.
- Contractions, slang, profanity level, emoji — and which ones.
- How they joke, if they do — dry asides, self-deprecation, none.
- How they ask for things — direct, hedged, playful.
- Pet words and phrases they reuse; words that never appear.

**Confirm.** Show the builder the tone profile and ask "is this how you sound?" Correct it before drafting.

**Apply.** Borrow habits, not sentences — never recycle the samples' content. Channel norms still set length, structure, and format; the tone profile sets word choice, rhythm, and register within them.

Boundaries:

- Only the builder's own writing. "Make me sound like [writer they admire]" produces imitation, which reads as fake as template copy — decline and interview instead.
- If samples are thin or missing, do more voice interview. Do not invent a persona from nothing.
- If a sample habit collides with a platform norm (all-lowercase in a LinkedIn post, say), keep the voice, flag the collision to the builder, and let them choose — don't silently normalize.

## Cut Tired Words

Hype words the builder wouldn't say out loud: "revolutionary," "game-changing," "seamless," "effortless," "supercharge," "unleash," "empower," "next-level."

Tech-writing tics that are worn smooth: "opinionated," "delightful," "blazingly fast," "batteries included," "just works," "beautifully simple," "powerful." These aren't wrong, they're exhausted — they mark copy as generated the same way "revolutionary" marks it as marketing.

"Opinionated" gets its own note because it keeps sneaking in: it labels a stance without stating one, and the label is the boring part. State the stance and it becomes the strongest line in the post.

- Weak: "The opinionated part: the playbook refuses vote requests."
- Better: "The playbook flat-out refuses to draft vote requests, undisclosed promotion, or DM blasts — and it never auto-posts."

Add the builder's own never-say words from the voice interview to this list.

## Do The Thing, Don't Announce The Thing

The playbook's rules — disclose, name a real weakness, follow community norms — are instructions for producing the copy. They must never surface in the copy as narration. A reader should see the behavior, not the checklist behind it.

- Disclosure is a plain sentence, not a described act. "I'm Tony — I built this" is a complete disclosure. "I'm Tony, the maker, disclosing upfront" narrates the compliance and reads like someone who was told to disclose.
- Honesty is stated, not labeled. Say the weakness ("output goes generic on thin source material"); don't caption it ("honest weakness:", "to be fully transparent").
- Rule-following shows up as fit — right format, right register, a post that's useful on its own — not as a claim ("I read the rules before posting").

Test: delete the meta-phrase and see if anything is lost. "Disclosing upfront," "to be honest," "in the spirit of transparency" — if the sentence still works without it, it was narration.

## Translate Playbook Vocabulary

The workflow's planning language — "permission," "proof fit," "borrowed relevance," "readiness gate," "audience map," "reply bank," "second wave" — belongs in planning files. In public copy it either confuses ("permission" from whom?) or reads as process being shown off. Translate every term into what it means for the reader:

- Weak: "an audience map scored by relevance AND permission."
- Better: "a list of communities scored by how relevant they are and whether their rules actually allow a launch post."

This leaks most when the product being launched is itself agent- or workflow-shaped, because the internal terms feel on-topic. They still need translating — the reader hasn't read the playbook.

Test: would a first-time reader with zero playbook context parse the sentence correctly on the first pass?

## Before Handing Back A Draft

1. Read it aloud, actually. Mark every phrase the builder wouldn't say to a peer.
2. Check it against the tone profile — openings, rhythm, punctuation, humor.
3. Sweep for tired words, narrated compliance, and untranslated playbook vocabulary.
4. Hand it back with open questions attached, per the co-writing loop. Tone corrections the builder makes are voice data — fold them back into the tone profile.
