# Co-Writing Guide

This is not a set of templates to fill in. Fill-in-the-blank launch copy reads as interchangeable AI slop - the placeholders get swapped and every post sounds the same. The goal here is the opposite: **draw the copy out of the builder** so it sounds like a real person who built a real thing.

Use this guide as an interview script and a set of guardrails, not a paste-ready form. It covers what to draw out of the builder; `tone-guide.md` covers how the finished copy should sound - read both before drafting.

## The Loop

For every channel, run the same three steps:

1. **Interview** - ask the questions below and listen for the specific, the surprising, and the honest. Pull from the Voice & Story section of `templates/launch-brief.md`.
2. **Draft from their words** - turn the builder's raw answers into a first draft that keeps their phrasing and their specifics, shaped to the channel's norms and length.
3. **Refine together** - hand the draft back with your open questions still attached. Revise until the builder says "yes, that's how I'd put it."

If you cannot make a line specific, that means you are missing input - ask for it. Do not paper over the gap with generic filler.

## Voice Interview (do this once, up front)

Before any channel-specific writing, capture how the builder actually talks:

- Paste the message you sent a friend when you first told them about this. (Their real register lives here.)
- Paste 3–10 pieces you've already written that sound like you - old launch posts, tweets or threads, README intros, blog paragraphs. (These go in the Reference Copy section of the launch brief; mine them into a tone profile per `tone-guide.md`.)
- What was the exact moment you decided to build it?
- What annoys you about how products like this are usually described?
- What's a word or phrase you'd never use about your own work?
- What are you quietly proud of that you'd be almost embarrassed to say out loud?

Record the answers in the launch brief. Every draft below should sound like these answers, not like marketing.

## Anti-Slop Guardrails (apply to every draft)

- Use the builder's real specifics - names, numbers, the actual origin moment - never a stand-in.
- Write to the builder's tone profile and the word rules in `tone-guide.md`: cut hype words and tired tech tics ("opinionated," "seamless," "game-changing"), never narrate a rule being followed ("disclosing upfront"), and translate playbook vocabulary ("permission," "readiness gate") into plain reader language.
- One concrete feedback ask per post, phrased as a real question the builder wants answered.
- Name a real limitation. A post with no honest weakness reads as an ad.
- If two channels' drafts could be swapped without anyone noticing, they're too generic - differentiate by what each audience actually cares about.
- Match length and register to the platform; do not paste the same block everywhere.
- Never add a vote request, fake urgency, or undisclosed-promotion framing.

## Product Hunt - First Comment

Interview for:

- Who is this embarrassingly *not* for? (the honest limitation)
- What are the two or three things it genuinely does today - in outcomes, not features?
- What's the one question you actually want makers to answer?

What good looks like: introduces the builder as a person, tells the real "why I built it," lists concrete capabilities as outcomes, states who it's not for, and ends with one specific feedback ask. No upvote request - ever.

## Show HN - Title And Post

Interview for:

- What's the plain, non-salesy name for what this is?
- What technical detail can an HN reader actually inspect or poke at?
- What did you try first that didn't work, and what did you do instead?
- What's transparent about pricing/access you should say up front?

What good looks like: a neutral, factual title (`Show HN: [what it is] - [plain detail]`); a post that states the concrete problem without sales language, explains how it works in inspectable terms, names what's different without superlatives, links to something tryable, is transparent about pricing, and invites a specific technical question. Write like a builder talking to builders.

## Reddit / Community - Title And Body

Interview for:

- Which specific community, and what did its rules say when you re-read them?
- Why is this genuinely relevant to *that* community (not just "it's my launch")?
- What's useful here even for someone who never uses the product - a lesson, benchmark, template, write-up, or comparison?

What good looks like: discloses the builder made it, shows they read the rules, leads with context and usefulness over the link, and asks one specific question. The builder stays in the comments. Re-read the community's current rules in-app right before posting.

## LinkedIn - Post

Interview for:

- What's the professional pain or business observation behind this?
- What did you actually learn while building it that a peer would find useful?
- Which role or persona do you most want feedback from?

What good looks like: professional framing, a genuine founder narrative, two or three outcomes it delivers today, one real lesson from building it, and a targeted feedback ask. Pre-schedulable - it doesn't need live hosting.

## X/Twitter - Sequence

Interview for:

- What's the single visual (GIF/screenshot) that shows the "aha"?
- The origin story in one breath.
- The one thing you most want feedback on.

What good looks like: a short sequence of small posts - the story with a visual, the old-way/new-way contrast with proof, and a specific feedback ask - each able to stand alone and invite replies. Spread across the day; reply when the builder surfaces from live threads.

## Direct Outreach - DM / Email

Interview for:

- Why *this* specific person? What did they ship, write, or say that connects?
- What's the honest, low-pressure ask?

What good looks like: opens with the real, specific reason they're relevant, states plainly what the builder made, makes a no-pressure ask for blunt feedback on one question, and includes the link. Personal beats polished. Never a copy-pasted blast.

## Newsletter / Podcast - Pitch

Interview for:

- What specific piece of theirs makes this a fit for their audience?
- What's the angle their audience would *learn* from - beyond "a product launched"?

What good looks like: references the specific work of the writer/host, connects it to their audience, frames the interesting angle (a lesson, technical approach, or story) rather than an announcement, and offers to send a founder note, screenshots, or answers.

## GitHub Release Notes

Interview for:

- What actually shipped in this version, in the builder's words?
- What are the honest limits a user should know before trying it?

What good looks like: a plain summary of what's inside, a clear first-run path, honest limits, and a real license/feedback channel. Sequence it before the Show HN / Product Hunt post so links resolve.
